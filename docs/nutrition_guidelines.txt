NUTRITION AND DIETARY GUIDELINES

Macronutrients
Carbohydrates should make up 45-65% of total daily calories. Choose complex carbohydrates such as whole grains, legumes, fruits, and vegetables over refined sugars. Protein should account for 10-35% of daily calories. Good protein sources include lean meats, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils, tofu, and nuts. Fats should comprise 20-35% of daily calories, with an emphasis on unsaturated fats from olive oil, avocados, nuts, and fatty fish. Limit saturated fats to less than 10% of daily calories and avoid trans fats entirely.

Fruits and Vegetables
Adults should eat at least 5 servings of fruits and vegetables per day. A serving is approximately one medium fruit, half a cup of cooked vegetables, or one cup of raw leafy greens. Aim for variety and include all color groups: red (tomatoes, bell peppers), orange (carrots, sweet potatoes), green (spinach, broccoli), blue/purple (blueberries, eggplant), and white (garlic, onions). Fruits and vegetables provide essential vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, and dietary fiber.

Fiber
Adults need 25-38 grams of dietary fiber per day. Women under 50 should aim for 25 grams; men under 50 should aim for 38 grams. Fiber supports digestive health, lowers cholesterol, regulates blood sugar, and helps maintain a healthy weight. High-fiber foods include oats, barley, lentils, beans, chickpeas, berries, apples, pears, broccoli, and whole grain breads and cereals.

Sodium
Limit sodium intake to less than 2,300 mg per day. Those with hypertension, kidney disease, or diabetes should aim for 1,500 mg or less. Most sodium in the diet comes from processed and restaurant foods, not the salt shaker. Read food labels carefully. High-sodium foods to limit include canned soups, processed meats, fast food, pickled foods, and soy sauce. Use herbs, spices, lemon juice, and vinegar to flavor foods instead of salt.

Sugar
Added sugars should make up no more than 10% of total daily calories, which is about 50 grams (12 teaspoons) for a 2,000-calorie diet. The American Heart Association recommends even stricter limits: no more than 25 grams (6 teaspoons) per day for women and 36 grams (9 teaspoons) for men. Reduce added sugar by choosing water over sugary drinks, eating whole fruit instead of juice, and selecting low-sugar versions of cereals, yogurts, and condiments.

Vitamins and Minerals
Calcium: Adults need 1,000-1,200 mg per day from dairy products, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, and almonds. Vitamin D: 600-800 IU daily from sunlight, fatty fish, fortified dairy, and supplements. Iron: 8-18 mg daily from red meat, poultry, seafood, beans, and fortified cereals. Vitamin B12: 2.4 mcg daily from animal products or supplements (critical for vegans and older adults). Folate: 400 mcg daily from legumes, leafy greens, and fortified grains.

Healthy Eating Patterns
The Mediterranean diet emphasizes plant-based foods, olive oil, fish, and moderate amounts of poultry and dairy. It is associated with reduced risk of heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes. The DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) is designed to lower blood pressure and focuses on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and low-fat dairy while limiting sodium, saturated fat, and sweets. Both patterns support overall health and disease prevention.
