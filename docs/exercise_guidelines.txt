EXERCISE AND PHYSICAL ACTIVITY GUIDELINES

Recommended Weekly Activity
Adults should aim for at least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise per week, OR 75-150 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity, OR an equivalent combination of both. Additionally, adults should perform muscle-strengthening activities involving all major muscle groups on 2 or more days per week. These recommendations come from the American Heart Association and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Physical Activity Guidelines.

Aerobic Exercise
Moderate-intensity aerobic activities include brisk walking, water aerobics, recreational cycling on flat terrain, doubles tennis, and ballroom dancing. Vigorous-intensity activities include running, swimming laps, singles tennis, aerobic dancing, and hiking uphill with a heavy backpack. The talk test is a simple way to gauge intensity: during moderate exercise you can talk but not sing; during vigorous exercise you cannot say more than a few words without pausing for breath.

Strength Training
Muscle-strengthening exercises include weight lifting, resistance band exercises, push-ups, pull-ups, sit-ups, yoga, and heavy gardening like digging. For building muscle and strength, perform 2-4 sets of 8-12 repetitions for each exercise. Allow at least 48 hours of recovery between training the same muscle group. Increase resistance progressively over time to continue making gains. Core strengthening exercises help prevent back pain and improve balance and stability.

Flexibility and Balance
Stretching improves flexibility and range of motion. Hold each stretch for 15-60 seconds without bouncing. Stretch major muscle groups 2-3 times per week, preferably after exercise when muscles are warm. Balance training is especially important for older adults to prevent falls. Effective balance exercises include standing on one leg, heel-to-toe walking, tai chi, and yoga. Even 10-15 minutes of stretching and balance work provides significant benefits.

Exercise for Specific Conditions
Heart disease: Low-to-moderate intensity aerobic exercise is beneficial; consult a cardiologist before starting. Diabetes: Regular exercise helps lower blood sugar; monitor glucose before and after exercise. Arthritis: Low-impact exercises like swimming, cycling, and walking reduce joint pain and stiffness. Osteoporosis: Weight-bearing exercises like walking, dancing, and strength training help maintain bone density. Hypertension: Regular aerobic exercise can lower blood pressure by 4-9 mmHg on average.

Starting an Exercise Program
If you are new to exercise or have a chronic health condition, consult your healthcare provider before starting. Begin gradually with 10-15 minute sessions and slowly increase duration and intensity over several weeks. Warm up for 5 minutes with light activity before exercise and cool down for 5 minutes afterward. Listen to your body and stop exercising if you experience chest pain, severe shortness of breath, dizziness, or joint pain. Wear appropriate footwear and use proper form to prevent injury.

Children and Adolescents
Children and adolescents aged 6-17 should get at least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity per day. Most of this should be aerobic activity such as running, swimming, or biking. Muscle-strengthening activities like gymnastics, climbing, and push-ups should be included at least 3 days per week. Bone-strengthening activities like jumping, hopscotch, and running should also occur at least 3 days per week. Limit sedentary screen time and encourage regular movement throughout the day.
