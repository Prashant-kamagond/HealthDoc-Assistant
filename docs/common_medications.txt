COMMON MEDICATIONS AND USAGE GUIDELINES

Over-the-Counter Pain Relievers
Acetaminophen (Tylenol): Used for mild to moderate pain and fever reduction. Standard adult dose is 325-650 mg every 4-6 hours, not exceeding 3,000-4,000 mg per day. Avoid alcohol while taking acetaminophen. Do not take if you have liver disease. Do not combine with other medications containing acetaminophen.

Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin): A nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used for pain, fever, and inflammation. Standard adult dose is 200-400 mg every 4-6 hours, not exceeding 1,200 mg per day without medical supervision. Take with food or milk to reduce stomach upset. Avoid if you have kidney disease, peptic ulcer disease, or are pregnant (especially in the third trimester). May increase blood pressure and cardiovascular risk with long-term use.

Naproxen (Aleve): An NSAID used for pain and inflammation. Standard adult dose is 220-440 mg every 8-12 hours, not exceeding 660 mg per day. Has a longer duration of action compared to ibuprofen. Similar precautions apply as with ibuprofen.

Aspirin: Used for pain, fever, and as a blood thinner. Standard adult dose for pain is 325-650 mg every 4-6 hours. Low-dose aspirin (81 mg daily) is used for cardiovascular protection in appropriate patients as directed by a doctor. Do not give aspirin to children or teenagers with viral illness due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.

Antihistamines
Diphenhydramine (Benadryl): A first-generation antihistamine used for allergies, hives, and as a sleep aid. Standard adult dose is 25-50 mg every 4-6 hours. Causes significant drowsiness. Do not drive or operate heavy machinery. Avoid in elderly patients due to increased risk of confusion and falls.

Loratadine (Claritin) and Cetirizine (Zyrtec): Second-generation antihistamines with less sedating effects. Standard dose is 10 mg once daily. Used for seasonal allergies, hives, and allergic rhinitis. Cetirizine may cause some drowsiness in certain individuals. Both are safe for long-term use.

Antacids and Digestive Medications
Calcium carbonate (Tums): An antacid used for heartburn and indigestion. Take 2-4 tablets as needed after meals and at bedtime. Also serves as a calcium supplement. Limit use to treat symptoms only; see a doctor for frequent or severe heartburn.

Omeprazole (Prilosec): A proton pump inhibitor (PPI) that reduces stomach acid production. Used for GERD, ulcers, and frequent heartburn. Take before meals. Intended for short-term use (2-4 weeks) without medical supervision; long-term use requires physician guidance due to risks including reduced magnesium absorption and bone density loss.

Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol): Used for nausea, heartburn, indigestion, and diarrhea. Avoid in children and teenagers with viral illness. Contains salicylate (related to aspirin) which may interact with blood thinners.

Cough and Cold Medications
Dextromethorphan (DXM): A cough suppressant found in many cold medicines. Used for dry, non-productive coughs. Do not take with MAO inhibitors. Do not exceed recommended doses.

Guaifenesin (Mucinex): An expectorant that helps thin and loosen mucus. Drink plenty of water while taking. Used for productive coughs with mucus.

Decongestants (Pseudoephedrine, Phenylephrine): Relieve nasal congestion. Pseudoephedrine may raise blood pressure. Avoid in patients with hypertension, heart disease, or thyroid disorders. Use for no longer than 3-5 days to avoid rebound congestion.

Medication Safety
Always read the full label before taking any medication. Store medications away from heat, moisture, and sunlight. Never share prescription medications. Dispose of unused medications properly using drug take-back programs. Inform your healthcare provider of all medications, supplements, and herbal products you take to avoid drug interactions. Do not exceed recommended doses.
